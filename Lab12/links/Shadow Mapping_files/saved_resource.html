                                                              JoeyDeVries Sensei Nights87 a year ago

                                                              Yeah, this happens because of shadow map precision issues and shadow bias that offsets the shadow a little and this is especially noticeable when a cube is on the floor. Because the cube touching the floor, its back-faces depth map values should directly correspond to the floor's shadow values, but as you can see that isn't always the case.
                                                              As with most of shadow mapping it's usually tiny little hacks that don't necessarily work on all objects; in this case it's best not to do face culling when a cube directly touches the ground (or find some other clever trick I can't think of at the top of my head ;) )

                                                                    JoeyDeVries Sensei Nights87 a year ago

                                                                    My guess is that in your original scene you have the culling order (or winding order of the vertices) reversed, in which case you cull the back-faces of all triangles which still results in the obvious edge you see there.

                                                                    Then my guess is that due to the inversion of the y-axis in the projection matrix, you also inverse the winding order of the vertices rendered, in which case you accidentily fixed the proper faces to cull.

                                                                    Instead of changing the orthographic projection, try changing the faces to cull (or check that the front faces are properly culled by culling them in your normal scene (with lights and perspective projection)).

                                                                    You can do this by either setting the faces to cull to GL_BACK or changing the 'to-cull-winding-order' of the vertices as follows:

                                                                    glCullFace(GL_BACK); 
// or ...
glFrontFace(GL_CW);

                                                                                          JoeyDeVries Sensei Owen Charette 24 days ago

                                                                                          You'd need PCF to blur the shadow maps (I've showed a very basic PCF example in this tutorial); there are much better ways to do PCF of which you can find plenty when you google around.

                                                                                          If you want a much better (and cheaper) blur I'd suggest you look into variance shadow mapping which is a great technique and largely similar to shadow mapping for high quality looking shadows where you can use the hardware's filtering solutions (like linear texture sampling) and use blurs like a gaussian blur onto the shadow map. Take a look at BennyBox's youtube tutorials about variance shadow mapping if you want to know more. The technique has a few caveats in where it won't always work (specifically with light bleeding), but so does shadow mapping :)
